ALUMNI HISTORY

In 1913, when Marinduque was still a sub-province of Tayabas, some American teachers came to Boac to organize secondary classes. Fewer than 20 students enrolled, so only a first-year class was organized. These pioneers included Candida Buñag, Francisco Lagula, Manuel Lagulo, Jose Lualhati, Pedro Licop, Clemente Montalan, Alfonsa Montellano, Pedro Navarro, Sixto Vitto, Eufemia Jardiniano, Ursula Lecaroz, Valentina Ledesma, Socario Nepomuceno, and Severina Vitta. They occupied one of the rooms in the old Municipal Building, which stood at the present Casa Real site, with Mr. Grover C. Sebastian as their teacher.
In 1914, 25 students enrolled under Mr. H. Heidmann. Mrs. Felisa Contreras-Lardizabal and Mrs. Salud Semilla Alcantara were two of the outstanding students in the class. Records for 1915 could not be located. However, in 1916, records show that only 14 students enrolled under Mr. Conrado Paras. Eufrocina Guevara Nepomuceno and Celestina Sarmiento dela Cerna were the outstanding students of that class.
Again, records for 1917 could not be found. In 1918, 33 students enrolled in the first year and 22 students in the second year under Mr. Aurelio Arguilles. The number of students increased to 72 in 1920 under Mr. Alfonso Villaster. In 1921, a certain Mr. de Vera was assigned as Handwork Teacher of Marinduque.
In 1922, Marinduque became a province and a Division Superintendent was assigned. News about the school traveled across the sea, and students from the neighboring province of Mindoro came to enroll. The present location in Isok, Boac was acquired, and a permanent building was constructed. However, the building was burned during the Japanese Occupation and was reconstructed after liberation by the War Damages Commission. In addition to the first and second year classes, a third-year class was organized with 22 students.
Due to the increase in enrollment, a survey was conducted to determine the possibility of opening a fourth-year class. Parents and the administration, backed by the Provincial Board, organized a fourth-year class in 1923 with 17 students. From then on, a complete high school existed in Boac. Enrollment continued to increase year after year until the outbreak of World War II.
When classes resumed after the war (1944–1945), enrollment almost doubled. Elementary pupils who were supposed to graduate in 1941, when World War II began, were automatically promoted to first year. There was a great demand for teachers. The shortage of classrooms was addressed by renting nearby buildings.
In 1946, the total population of Marinduque High School reached 554 students; 126 of them graduated that year under Principal Juan L. Manuel, who later became Secretary of Education. The population continued to increase until 1951, after which it gradually declined due to the establishment of private schools in different towns of the province.
A full-scale improvement program was initiated in 1956 under the leadership of Ex-Congressman Pamfilo Manguera (Class of 1927) and Mrs. Elena Jambalos, who was then the principal of the school. The makeshift building constructed through the resourcefulness of Mr. Gregorio Punzalan, the school principal from 1948–1949, was twice blown down by Typhoons Juan and Trix. The building was finally demolished when Congressman Pamfilo Manguera gave his alma mater a substantial portion of his pork barrel funds for the construction of the Home Economics and Shop Buildings.
Permanent improvements were made through the help of teachers, students, alumni, and other civic-spirited individuals. Mr. Bernardo Jambalos Jr., for one, showed unlimited support, which was largely instrumental in the speedy improvements made to the school since 1955.
The inspiring practice of selecting an Outstanding Alumnus was instituted by Mrs. Jambalos in 1955. Ex-Congressman Manguera was unanimously chosen as the first Outstanding Alumnus and received a gold medal donated by Chief Justice Ricardo Paras of the Supreme Court. This began the tradition of awarding outstanding alumni annually during the graduation ceremonies.
As the school continued to earn recognition and prestige for its competence as an educational institution through the quality of its graduates, it also gained support from people both within and outside the province. Such support came in the form of increasing student enrollment, donations of books and school equipment, and the construction of additional classrooms.
Around 1965, the construction of the Lecaroz Building began on the east side of the campus. In the early 1970s, the PTA donated a two-story building with four classrooms in the west area, which was named the De La Santa Building.
Shortly thereafter, under the Marcos administration, the Bagong Lipunan Building was added beside the De La Santa Building in 1982. Assemblywoman Carmencita O. Reyes sponsored and worked for the approval of Parliamentary Bill No. 1328, which proposed the nationalization of Marinduque High School. The bill was signed into law by President Ferdinand Marcos on November 13, 1982, under Batas Pambansa Blg. 267. However, the law took effect only in August 1984 when the first cash disbursement ceiling (BCDC) was released. Under Principal Martin Adata, the school officially adopted its new name, Marinduque National High School.
